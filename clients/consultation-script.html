Script

5Phases

0Hard Pitches
Core principle: you are not selling — you are guiding the client to sell themselves. Every question is designed to get them talking. The more they articulate the problem and the cost of inaction, the more they commit. Your role is to listen, reflect, and at the end connect what they said to what you offer.
Phase 01
Before You Touch a Bar
5 min
Setup
Sit down face to face before you go anywhere near the gym floor. A table or bench works — somewhere that says "conversation," not "assessment." No clipboard, no form, no rushing to warm up. The session starts here. How you run this 5 minutes signals the entire dynamic: you're in control, you're thoughtful, and you care about more than just the workout.
Open with
Question 1 — The Trigger
"What made you reach out now — what changed recently?"
Don't ask "what's your goal." You'll get a vague answer like "lose weight" or "get stronger" that tells you nothing useful. This question surfaces the trigger event — the specific thing that pushed them from thinking about training to actually contacting you. That's where real motivation lives. Listen for: a health scare, a bad photo, a birthday, a breakup, a new job, watching someone else get results. Whatever they say — that's what you come back to at the close.
Question 2 — The Timeline
"How long have you been thinking about this?"
This is a readiness probe. Two years of circling means a lot of accumulated frustration — which is a lot of energy available. Two weeks means fresh motivation but not yet conviction. Either way the answer tells you how to calibrate your close. Longer timeline = lean on the "what's stopped you" angle. Shorter = lean on momentum.
Question 3 — The Real Goal
"When you imagine things going really well — what does that actually look like? Not just the number on the scale or the bar — what changes day to day?"
People have a surface goal and a real goal. Surface: "lose 10kg." Real: "feel confident," "keep up with my kids," "not be out of breath on stairs." The real goal carries emotional weight. Use their exact language from this answer in your close — it lands differently than generic benefit-speak.
What you're listening for
A specific triggering event — not just a vague desire
Emotional language — frustration, embarrassment, hope
How long they've been circling without acting
Whether motivation is pain-driven or aspiration-driven
Lifestyle details — work, stress, schedule constraints
Whether they researched you specifically or just any PT
Phase 02
Surface the Cost of Staying the Same
5–8 min
The logic
Most trainers immediately pitch benefits: "you'll feel stronger, have more energy, sleep better." That's fine but it's weak. Benefits are future and hypothetical. Pain is present and real. This phase gets the client to articulate — in their own words — what the cost of not changing looks like. You don't say it. They do. Once they've said it out loud, the case for action is already made before you've offered anything.
Questions
The Cost Question
"What happens if nothing changes in the next 6 months?"
Don't rush past this. Ask it, then stop talking. Let the silence sit. The discomfort is doing your work. Most people have never been asked to articulate the cost of inaction directly — when they do, it lands harder than anything you could say about your service.
The History Question
"Have you tried to sort this before — on your own or with someone else? What happened?"
Past failed attempts are gold. If they've tried and it didn't stick, they already know at some level that they need external structure or expertise. You don't have to argue that point — they've already proven it to themselves. Acknowledge it simply: "That's really common — it's exactly why most people who get results work with someone."
The Obstacle Question
"What's actually stopped you from doing this before?"
This is the most important question in the whole consultation. The answer is the real objection — not the one they'll give you at the close, but the deeper one underneath. Common answers: time, money, not knowing what to do, fear of injury, lack of motivation, a bad experience before. Note it. Your close needs to address it directly.
Strong buy-in signals
  • Mentions time already wasted
  • Emotional when describing the goal
  • Very specific about what's not working
  • Has tried multiple approaches already
  • Brings up the problem unprompted
  • Asks you questions about your approach
Proceed with caution
  • Vague or shrugging answers
  • Asking about price before anything else
  • "Just want to try it out"
  • No clear trigger for reaching out
  • Distracted, disengaged
  • Someone else pushed them to come
Phase 03
The Session
45–50 min
Your goal
This is your proof of concept. They're evaluating you as much as they're evaluating training. Coach with full attention — cue technique, explain reasoning briefly, keep intensity appropriate for a first session. Don't go easy to be liked, and don't destroy them to prove a point. Leave them tired but not wrecked, and already feeling like they learned something they didn't know before.
01
Cue early and oftenMost people have never had movement corrected in real-time. One well-delivered technical cue they've never heard before makes you stand out from every YouTube workout they've done alone.
02
Name the "why" briefly"The reason we do this before that is..." or "Most people skip this but it matters because..." — brief reasoning signals expertise without lecturing. Keep it to one sentence.
03
Reference their goalConnect exercises to what they told you in Phase 01. "This directly relates to what you mentioned about posture" — they feel heard and coached, not processed.
04
Control the paceRushed sessions feel chaotic. Calm, deliberate pacing signals that you're experienced and that working with you would be well-structured — not just hard.
Seed lines
Drop 2–3 lines during the session that plant the idea of continuity — without pitching. These feel like natural coaching comments, not sales talk. Say them during rest periods, not mid-set.
Seed 1 — Progression
"This is the kind of thing that compounds quickly — in 4–6 weeks, this same movement would feel completely different."
Plants the idea of a longer arc without making it a sales line.
Seed 2 — Structure
"The reason I program it this way is [brief reason] — most people in commercial gyms never get this level of structure. It makes a bigger difference than people expect."
Differentiates you from self-guided training without directly criticising it.
Seed 3 — Personalisation
"Based on what you told me earlier, this [movement / pattern / habit] is probably the biggest lever for you specifically."
Shows you were listening in Phase 01. This one carries a lot of weight — it makes the session feel personalised, not generic.
Phase 04
Commitment Reveal
5 min — post-session
Timing
Ask this while they're cooling down, grabbing water, or towelling off — not sitting formally across from you. Casual setting gets a more honest answer. The question is simple but precise: it filters readiness, reveals the real objection if there is one, and creates self-commitment all in one move.
The question
Scale Question
"On a scale of 1 to 10 — how important is sorting this out to you right now?"
Ask it clean — don't soften it or add explanation. Then wait for the number. Whatever they say, your next move is determined by the response tree below.

Phase 05
The Close
5–10 min
The method
You're not closing a sale — you're connecting the dots between what they told you and what you can do about it. Use their exact words: the trigger from Phase 01, the cost they described in Phase 02, their specific goal. When they hear their own language reflected back with a solution attached, the decision feels obvious rather than pressured.
Don't say
"Would you like to sign up for PT sessions?"
Say instead
"Based on what you told me, here's exactly what I'd do with you. Does that make sense as a next step?"
The difference
One asks them to buy. The other proposes a solution to their specific stated problem.
Close script
Fill in the brackets with their exact words from Phases 01 and 02
"You mentioned that [trigger — what changed recently] and that you've been trying to sort [their specific goal] for [their timeframe] — and it hasn't moved. Based on what we did today and what you told me, I have a clear picture of where to start and what the biggest levers are for you. Here's what I'd do: [2–3 sentence outline — specific, not generic]. Does that feel like the right direction?"
Handling objections
Don't pre-empt objections by over-explaining your offer. Wait for them to surface, then respond to the specific one raised — not all possible ones at once. Volunteering extra justifications signals insecurity and can introduce doubts that weren't there.
They say What it really means Your response
"I need to think about it." One unresolved question they haven't said out loud yet. "Of course — what specifically do you need to think through? Maybe I can answer it now."
"It's a bit expensive." Haven't connected the cost to the value yet. Or comparing to the wrong thing. "That's fair — what are you comparing it to? I want to make sure we're looking at it the same way."
"I'm not sure I have the time." Fear of commitment or failure, not actual schedule conflict. "How many days a week would feel realistic without adding stress? We can absolutely work with that."
"I want to try on my own first." Hasn't fully trusted you yet, or hasn't acknowledged past solo failures. "That makes sense — you mentioned you tried that before though. What would be different this time?"
"Can I start next month?" Momentum is there but looking for permission to delay. "You could — but you said you've already been waiting [timeframe]. What changes next month?"
"I need to check with my partner." Usually price. Sometimes genuine. Surfaces a second decision-maker. "Of course. What do you think their main question will be? I can make sure you have what you need to explain it."
Follow-up
If they don't commit on the day, get a specific next step before they leave — not a vague "I'll follow up." Ambiguity kills conversions. Two follow-ups max — after that you're chasing, not converting.
If they leave undecided
"I'll send you a brief outline of what I'd structure for you — so you've got something concrete to look at."
Gives you a reason to follow up and positions it as delivering value, not chasing.
Follow-up message (24–48 hrs)
"Good session yesterday. Here's a rough outline of the direction I'd take with you based on what you told me. Let me know if it resonates or if you have questions."
Short. No pressure. Delivers something useful. Easy to respond to.
If no reply after 4–5 days
"Just checking in — any questions on what I sent over?"
One more touch. If still nothing — move on. Two is the limit before it becomes chasing.
If they say "not yet"
"No problem. I'll check back in [timeframe] — anything useful I can send in the meantime?"
Keeps the door open without pressure. Offering something useful keeps you top of mind positively.
Core Rule
You're not selling a PT package. You're solving a specific stated problem. Every phase of this script is designed to get the client to articulate their own problem clearly — because once they've said it out loud, you no longer have to argue for the value of solving it. The close is just you connecting what they already said to what you do. If the fit isn't genuine, don't force it. Bad-fit clients cost more than they're worth in time, energy, and reputation.
